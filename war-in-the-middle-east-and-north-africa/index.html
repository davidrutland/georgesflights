The Wartime Travels and Adventures of Flight Sgt. George Heritage

Through the middle east, North Africa, and Europe.

A collection of wartime photos taken while on duty and at leisure in the hot places of the world. George was based on Malta and in Egypt, acting as a rear gunner in a Wellington bomber as it attacked Axis installations in North Africa, Italy, and the occupied Greek Islands.

A rare picture of George driving. The vehicle is stationary and there is a man squatting on the bonnet, so who knows?

Chilling in Cairo. George is on the left; the other man is possibly Corporal Gordon

A postcard portrait of members of 148 Squadron, 338 Wing. The text on the back of the photo reads:

Inky, Seatson,

A Wellington Bomber - heavily used. Note the chipped paintwork, flat tyre, and that a large section of the engine is missing. This picture was probably taken during the double engine change at Kabrit between June 4th and 9th 1942.

A blurry shot of a Wellington bomber crew by their aircraft in the Egyptian desert. Sgt. George Heritage is front row left.

A group of servicemen outside the Empire club, Cairo. George is second from left. The text on the back of the photo reads:

Cairo November /42

Dick, Myself, Bill, Billy Barr

Taken outside “Empire” Club

McMinn

R.G. 10. (Form)

Dick is Dick Fenner. George and Dick were friends in Liverpool - living on opposite sides of the road - who joined up at the same time. Dick signed up with the army, and they did not see each other again, until this chance meetup in Cairo.

Being at war for three years, doesn’t mean you stop appreciating culture. Here is George Heritage after surveying Cairo from the summit of the great pyramid in January 1942.

20/1/42 Tired out after climbing to the top of the largest pyramid. Lots of love. George XXXXXX XXX. The picture was taken a week before starting active operations against Ey Zuetina

Fun fact: In early 1942, 148 Squadron “achieved the dubious, and possibly unique, distinction of having all its NCO aircrew flying operationally whilst on charges of insubordination, with five of them ultimately being court-martialed.” Source: Trusty to the End: The History of 148 (Special Duties) Squadron 1918-1945 by Oliver Clutton-Brock
